# Chapter 3 — Storage Engines: Debrief

## Two engines, one workload

You drove an **LSM-tree** and a **B-tree** with the exact same keys and watched three
numbers diverge: write amplification, read amplification, and space amplification. Neither
engine is "faster" — each pays for its strength somewhere you can measure.

## Why LSM writes are cheap but reads pay

The LSM-tree never seeks. Every write lands in an in-memory **memtable** (durably logged to
the WAL first), and when the memtable fills it is flushed as one sorted **SSTable** run —
a single sequential write. That is why writes are cheap.

Reads pay for it. A key could live in the memtable, or any L0 run, or L1 — so a point read
may probe several runs newest-first. That is **read amplification**. The **bloom filter** on
each run is the escape hatch: a probabilistic "definitely not here" that lets a read skip a
run without scanning it. You watched bloom skips climb — every skip is a disk read the LSM
*didn't* have to do.

## Compaction is the price of sequential writes

Sorted runs pile up, so the engine merges them: L0 runs collapse into L1, newest value wins,
and tombstones are dropped at the bottom level. That merge **rewrites data you already
wrote** — the source of LSM **write amplification**.

Here is the subtlety the scoreboard makes concrete: write-amp is **workload-dependent**.
Bulk-load a stream of *fresh* keys and the LSM compacts them roughly once (~2×) — while the
tiny-order B-tree splits a page on almost every insert, and *its* number is higher. But
**overwrite the same keys repeatedly** and the picture flips: every LSM compaction rewrites
those same live keys again, so its write-amp climbs past the B-tree's. Compaction is the
cost, and churn is what makes you pay it.

## Why B-tree reads are shallow but writes are never free

The B-tree keeps every key in exactly one place, in a balanced tree of fixed-fanout pages.
A read is one root-to-leaf walk — **read amplification = height**, shallow and near-constant,
which is why its read-amp barely moved while the LSM's wandered. You saw height grow by one
only when the root itself split.

But every write is a random in-place page write **plus** a WAL append — a flat floor of ~2×
that never goes away, and page splits add more on top. The B-tree trades write cost for read
predictability; the LSM trades read cost for write throughput.

## What the WAL saved

When you crashed both engines mid-write, the acknowledged key came back — on *both* sides.
Neither engine trusts its in-memory or half-written state after a crash; both replay the
**write-ahead log** to rebuild. What was lost was only the volatile part: an un-flushed
memtable, an uncommitted split page. The log is the durability boundary — everything before
the ack survives, everything after it is fair game.

The **torn write** made the other half of the point: a partially-written run is *flagged*,
not silently served as truth, and `recover` rebuilds it from the WAL. A checksum is what
turns silent corruption into a detectable fault — the difference between wrong answers and an
error.

## Space vs availability, under disk-full

Fill the disk and the two engines fail differently. The LSM keeps accepting writes but its
compaction **stalls** — it needs headroom to merge and doesn't have it — so L0 piles up and
read-amp climbs. The B-tree **refuses the split** outright. One degrades, one rejects: the
same disk-full pressure, two philosophies about what to give up.

## The trade-off you just measured

Write-optimised vs read-optimised is not a slogan — it is the compaction bytes and the page
traversals you counted, and which one wins depends on your workload.

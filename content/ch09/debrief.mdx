# Chapter 9 — Consistency & Consensus: Debrief

## The minority's silence IS the safety

When you partitioned the leader away, its side did not fail loudly — it went mute. The
write you issued there sat `pending` forever: with one vote out of five, the old leader
could never commit it. That silence is the entire safety argument. A value is decided
only when a majority holds it, and any two majorities overlap in at least one node — so
the two sides of a partition can never decide differently. The minority pays in
availability; in exchange, nothing it says can ever be wrong.

## Terms are fencing tokens

Ch8's fencing token came back wearing a new name. Every election increments the term,
every message carries it, and any node that hears a higher term steps down on the spot.
The deposed leader that answered your read was already fenced by the majority's new
term — its tragedy was answering the client *before hearing about it*. Same monotonic
number, same job: make stale authority provably stale.

## The one subtle rule: §5.4.2

A Raft leader only counts replication of entries *from its own term* toward commitment;
older entries commit implicitly when a current-term entry commits on top of them.
Without this restriction, a new leader could "commit" an old-term entry that a later
leader is still entitled to overwrite — and a committed entry must never be overwritten.
Your heal-and-repent challenge walked the edge of this rule: the isolated leader's
dangling write was never committed, so when it rejoined and its log conflicted with the
new leader's, truncating that tail away was legal. Lost, not corrupted — the client saw
`lost`, never a false `ok`.

## Linearizability, as the checker sees it

A history is linearizable if there exists one total order of its operations that (a)
respects real time — an operation that finished before another began must come first —
and (b) reads like a single register: every read returns the latest write before it.
The checker you clicked is a Wing–Gong search: it tries every seating order consistent
with real-time precedence (memoized, capped at 12 ops — the problem is NP-complete).
Your stale read broke clause (b) in every possible order: the new leader's write had
completed *before* your read began, yet the read returned the old value. No seating
exists; verdict `violation`; the culprit row is the read that cannot be seated.

## Reading fast without lying

The stale read happened because the old leader answered from local state without
checking it was still leader. The fix menu real systems pick from: **read-index** — the
leader confirms leadership with one heartbeat round before answering (one network round
trip, no log write); **lease reads** — the leader answers freely inside a clock-bounded
lease (fast, but now you trust clocks again — hello, Ch8); **quorum reads** — ask a
majority and take the freshest answer (always safe, always expensive).

## What was cut

Real Raft also ships **log compaction / snapshots** (the log cannot grow forever),
**membership change** (joint consensus for adding/removing nodes), and **PreVote** (a
rejoining node probes before incrementing terms, so a flapping node cannot depose a
healthy leader). None change the safety story you just drove.

## Real systems

**etcd** runs this exact protocol — read-index reads by default. **ZooKeeper** runs ZAB,
a sibling: same epochs-as-terms, same majority commit. **Spanner** runs Paxos per shard
with TrueTime-bounded lease reads — Google's answer to "lease reads, but the clock
uncertainty is measured, not assumed."
